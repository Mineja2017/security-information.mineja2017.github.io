## Shavers & Bair 2016: Hiding Behind the Keyboard: The Tor Browser €

**Core functionality**: Is a free modified browser that hides your IP among other things. It makes online tracing "almost imposible".
**Origins and use**: Originally developed by the US government in 2002 for secure communication, it is now open-source. While used legitimately by for example citizens avoiding censorship, it is also exploited by criminals.
**How it Works**: It enroutes the traffic through 3 random relays. Data is wrapped in layers of encryption—like an onion. Each relay only peels back one layer to see the next destination, ensuring no single relay knows the entire route.
**System vulnerabilities**: Breaking Tor cryptography is really complicated. It some times had been broken but using specialized browser exploits.
**Human element**: The biggest weakness of Tor is user error. Suspects are frequently caught because they tweak browser security settings, allow tracking scripts, open downloaded documents insecurely, or accidentally use a standard, non-Tor browser.
**How criminals get catch**: Investigators often catch suspects by tricking them into revealing their true IP address (using tracking codes in emails or files), connecting Dark Web aliases to open-web identities (as seen in the Silk Road case), or matching internal network access logs to the exact timing of a crime (as seen in the Harvard bomb threat case).
